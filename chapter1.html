LLMs 101: A Crash Course

Your first step into the world of Large Language Models

What is an LLM?

Large Language Models (LLMs) are deep neural networks trained on massive text corpora to understand and generate human language. Think of them as statistical models that predict the next word in a sequence. Unlike traditional rule-based systems, LLMs learn patterns from data through transformer architectures—neural networks that use attention mechanisms to process sequences in parallel rather than sequentially.

The "large" in LLM refers to both parameter count (typically hundreds of millions to trillions) and dataset size (often terabytes of text). Modern LLMs like GPT-4 contain billions of parameters—the weights and biases that encode learned patterns. These parameters are trainable coefficients that determine how input features influence outputs, similar to synaptic strengths in biological neural networks.

How Do They Work?

At their core, LLMs work by predicting the next word in a sequence. When you give them a prompt like "The weather today is", they analyze the context and predict what word should come next - perhaps "sunny", "rainy", or "cold".

This prediction is based on patterns they learned during training from massive amounts of text data. The more data they're trained on, and the more parameters they have, the better they become at making these predictions.

Key Components:

  • • Input Embeddings: Convert words to numerical representations
  • • Positional Encoding: Give the model information about word order
  • • Multi-Head Attention: Allow the model to focus on different parts of the input
  • • Feed-Forward Networks: Process the attended information
